AI Literacy, while really GenAI Literacy

Terms, Terms, Terms

  • AI = artificial intelligence

    • Models that try to predict outcomes.

  • GenAI = AI models that produce text, images, and videos in response to prompts.

  • LLMs = large language models

    • Can produce text that is very similar to human written text.

How are LLMs so good at producing human-like text?

  • Use neural networks, a very flexible type of AI model.

  • Built on very, very large datasets.

Result: A tool that takes virtually all the data on the internet and predicts what it thinks you want to say.

Prompt Engineering

  • Prompt: The input you give the LLM to get a response.

  • Spend time figuring out the problem you are trying to solve.

  • Clean prompts tend to contain:

    • A task
    • A format
    • A voice
    • Context

Example

  • Identify the task, format, voice, and any context.

  • What do you think?

Iterate!

  • Better prompts yield better outcomes.

  • Need to practice and refine our prompts to achieve better outcomes from GenAI.

  • Regardless of how good your prompt is, make sure to always verify the accuracy of the outcome!

Beware

LLMs give plausible results and rarely admit doubt or lack of knowledge.

Dealing with AI Hallucinations

  • AI isn’t much of a time saver if we need to verify all of its claims.

  • Verification goes much faster if you already have domain knowledge and have developed your critical thinking skills.

AI and the Classroom

“AI promises compelling benefits—such as saving time in busy lives and helping your work to appear more creative or clever—but there are many important but invisible tradeoffs in using it, especially in the classroom. Therefore, at least in school, there’s good reason not to use or rely on AI/LLMs while you’re still learning new skills and growing as a person, even if it’s ok to use it in future jobs where producing work is more important than learning.” – Patrick Lin, Philosophy Professor at Cal Poly


  • Make sure you are having conversations with your professors about what is and is not appropriate use of GenAI in your classes.
    • Policies will vary from class to class.

Ethical Risks

  • When you do use GenAI, make sure to credit GenAI contributions in your work through appropriate citations.

  • You should evaluate the relevance, usefulness, and accuracy of all GenAI outputs.

  • Investigate the potential harms of GenAI.
    • On the labor market.
    • On society.
    • On the environment.

Ethical Risks

  • Examine the privacy and data security risks of GenAI.
    • No confidentiality between you and most AI tools.

Thursday, November 6th noon - 12:50pm, Taylor 208, Food provided

Generative AI tools are a game-changer for complex data science projects. They can accelerate a project from 0 to 80% complete in a remarkably short amount of time, but the final 20% still demands the skills of a human data scientist to create a robust, nuanced, and context-specific solution.

In this talk, I will walk through several projects I have completed with the help of large language models. My goal is to help you think through the role of the human in data science work and the AI skills you should be developing while you are here at Bucknell and how these skills will prepare you to adapt and stay ahead in the rapidly evolving world after you leave Bucknell.
